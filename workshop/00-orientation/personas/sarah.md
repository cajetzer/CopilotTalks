## Formative Experiences

**The Silverlight Sprint (2011)**
Sarah's team spent six months rebuilding a customer portal in Microsoft Silverlight after management read a Gartner report and declared it "the future of enterprise UI." She warned them. She got overruled. The day she finished the migration, Microsoft announced Silverlight was being deprecated. She spent another four months migrating back. She has never again adopted a technology without seeing it survive at least two annual conference cycles first. This is where the skepticism became policy, not personality.

**The Code Review That Saved the Quarter (2018)**
A junior engineer on her team pushed a change that looked fine in review but introduced a subtle race condition under load. It hit production on a Friday. Sarah spent the weekend debugging it, eventually tracing it to a pattern she'd flagged in her informal style guide but never formalized. The fix took 45 minutes. The postmortem took three hours. That Monday she wrote her first formal coding standards document — 12 pages, enforced from that point forward via PR checklist. She references this event whenever someone asks why she cares so much about standards: *"Because I've paid the weekend tax for vague guidelines."*

**The Vendor Demo (2023)**
A SaaS vendor gave a flawless 45-minute AI coding demo to her leadership team. Impressive output, perfect narrative, standing ovation. Sarah asked one question afterward: *"Can you show me the failure rate on the generated code in your own production environment?"* The room went quiet. The vendor pivoted to a slide about "the future of developer productivity." Her CTO looked at her and said, "fair point." The tool was not purchased. This is why she doesn't trust demos — she trusts metrics.

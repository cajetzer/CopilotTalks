## Formative Experiences

**The Three-Hour Onboarding (2021)**
Marcus joined a new team and it took him three hours to get his local environment running. The README was six months out of date, the Node version was wrong, two environment variables weren't documented, and the database seed script silently failed without a specific version of Docker. He fixed everything, then spent an afternoon writing an automated setup script that reduced onboarding to 12 minutes. Six months later it had been adopted as the team standard and he'd received more Slack praise for it than any feature he'd shipped. He decided that day that *setup friction is the hidden tax on every developer's first week*, and eliminating it was more valuable than most features.

**The CI Pipeline That Ate Fridays (2022)**
Marcus inherited a CI pipeline that took 47 minutes to run and failed flakily about 30% of the time on a specific integration test nobody fully understood. Every Friday afternoon, someone got blocked by it. He spent three weeks profiling, parallelizing, and mocking the brittle dependency. Pipeline dropped to 11 minutes. Flake rate dropped to under 2%. The team started shipping on Fridays again. His manager called it "the highest-ROI engineering work of the quarter." Marcus framed the before/after numbers and still has them. He now talks about CI as *"the team's shared nervous system"* — when it's slow or unreliable, everyone suffers and nobody admits how much.

**The Hacker News Tab (ongoing)**
Marcus has had a Hacker News tab open in his browser since 2020. It's always in the background. He's read about more technologies there than he's actually used, and he's made peace with the ratio. His manager once joked that Marcus knows about every tool six months before the team needs it and forgets it exists three months after. Marcus doesn't disagree. He describes it as "passive reconnaissance" — most of it never matters, but occasionally something important surfaces and he's the only one in the room who's already read the docs.

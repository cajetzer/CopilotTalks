## Background

- Staff engineer, has seen technologies come and go
- Built his career on syntax mastery and deep language knowledge
- Remembers when knowing obscure language corners made you invaluable
- Skeptical of tools that claim to replace expertise

## Formative Experiences

**The Junior Who Didn't Need Him (2019)**
David mentored a junior developer named Priya for eight months. She was sharp, curious, asked great questions. Then she started using a new AI-assisted coding tool and stopped asking questions. Not because she stopped learning — because the tool was answering faster than David could. He noticed it first when she closed a GitHub issue he'd been about to comment on, with a solution better than the one he'd been composing. He felt a flash of something he didn't immediately recognize as pride. It took him a week to realize it wasn't a threat — it was the point. He'd spent eight months transferring knowledge; the tool had just become another vector. But the flash was real, and he still thinks about it.

**The Design Pattern Argument (2015)**
At a previous company, David spent three days in architecture review defending a decision to use the Saga pattern for a distributed transaction problem. His counterpart wanted a simpler two-phase commit. David won the argument. The Saga implementation took four months to stabilize in production. Two years later, the pattern was table stakes in the industry. He uses this story to explain his operating principle: *"I'm not pedantic because I enjoy it. I'm pedantic because 'good enough for now' has a compounding interest rate."* He's been right enough times that people stopped arguing and started asking.

**The Conference That Changed His Mind About "Junior Code" (2021)**
David gave a talk at a regional conference on language-level optimization patterns. Excellent talk — he knew his material cold. Afterward, a developer who looked about 24 years old showed him a project that solved the same class of problem with a third of the code using a library David had never heard of. The library was two years old. David had missed it entirely. He flew home and spent the weekend auditing his blind spots. He still thinks deep expertise matters — maybe more than ever — but he thinks about it differently now: *expertise is the map; you still have to check if the territory has changed.*

## Goals

- Understand if Copilot is genuinely useful or just fancy autocomplete
- Find ways AI can enhance (not replace) his architectural thinking
- Stay current without abandoning hard-earned expertise

## Fears

- **That AI will make his expertise obsolete** — If anyone can generate code, what's his value?
- **Being replaced by junior developers with AI tools** — Will companies decide they don't need expensive senior engineers?
- **Losing the craft** — Programming as an intellectual pursuit, not just prompt engineering
- **His knowledge becoming a liability** — Patterns he knows deeply might be "automated away"

## Voice in Exercises

Precise to a fault — he'll correct your framing before engaging with your question. Genuinely impressed by things that hold up under scrutiny, genuinely dismissive of things that don't. His concerns aren't obstruction; they're quality control with 20 years of pattern recognition behind them.

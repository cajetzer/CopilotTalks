## Background

- Constantly balancing stakeholder requests, technical debt, and innovation
- Speaks both "business" and "technical" fluently

## Formative Experiences

**The Sprint He Couldn't Explain (2018)**
Rafael was a developer on a team that spent three weeks building a feature nobody wanted. The requirements were clear. The spec was complete. The implementation was technically correct. At the demo, the product manager — the person Rafael now is — looked at it and said, *"This isn't what the users asked for."* Rafael had never talked to a user. The PM hadn't talked to the team. The spec had been written in a vacuum and accepted in a vacuum. He volunteered to switch teams to work directly under that PM. Six months later he was running product for a small feature set. A year after that he'd transitioned full-time. He still thinks like a developer — he just now thinks about *why* something is being built before *what* gets built.

**The Stakeholder Who Moved The Goalposts (2021)**
A VP of Sales asked Rafael's team for a "simple export feature" that would take, by his estimate, two days. Rafael's team scoped it at three weeks. The VP escalated. Rafael built a one-page technical brief — no jargon, costs-benefits, three implementation options with tradeoff tables — and walked the VP through it in 20 minutes. The VP chose the two-week option and stopped escalating. Rafael has written some version of that brief for every significant requirement since. He calls it *"the translation layer"* — not dumbing things down, but ensuring that decision-makers have the information they need in the format they can use.

**The OKR That Nobody Shipped To (2023)**
Rafael's team had a quarterly OKR: *"Improve developer experience by 20%."* At the end of the quarter, three features had shipped, two were half-done, and nobody could agree on whether the OKR had been hit because nobody had defined what "20% better" meant. He spent the next month working with engineering leads to define measurable outcomes before any future OKR was written. He now refuses to write a requirement that doesn't include an acceptance criterion expressible as a number. His developers initially complained this added overhead. The following quarter, for the first time in two years, the team agreed they'd hit their goals.

- Creating requirements that sound good but are vague or contradictory
- Losing touch with the technical details that inform good product decisions
- AI-generated specs that miss the nuance of what users actually need
